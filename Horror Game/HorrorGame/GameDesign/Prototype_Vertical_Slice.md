# Prototype Vertical Slice

The first playable prototype should prove the entire game concept with one complete dream-to-reality loop.

Use the hallway dream first because it directly connects to the bedroom, sleep, memory, and the house routine.

## Prototype Flow

1. Player wakes up in bedroom.
2. Player checks TV.
3. Player checks answering machine.
4. Player turns off kitchen light.
5. Player goes to bed.
6. Player enters endless hallway.
7. Player solves object-memory puzzle.
8. Player reaches bedroom door.
9. Player wakes up.
10. Bedroom photo has changed.
11. Answering machine has new message.
12. Closet is now unlocked.

## House Tasks

The house should start simple and readable:

- Check TV.
- Check answering machine.
- Turn off kitchen light.
- Go to bed.

The chores should feel ordinary at first. The horror comes from repetition, small changes, and the player realizing the house is responding to dreams.

## Dream Puzzle

The first dream is the endless bedroom hallway.

Object order:

1. Fix upside-down painting.
2. Turn off flickering lamp.
3. Answer ringing phone.
4. Stop backward clock.
5. Cover mirror.

After each correct interaction, the bedroom door gets closer. Wrong interactions reset the hallway.

## Wake-Up Consequences

After the dream:

- Bedroom photo changes.
- Answering machine has a new strange message.
- Closet unlocks.
- Bedroom door is slightly open.
- Bedsheets are messy.

## Prototype Success Criteria

This slice is successful if it proves:

- The house routine works.
- The player can enter a dream through sleep.
- The dream has a readable puzzle.
- The dream sets story flags.
- The house changes after waking.
- The new house changes unlock story or exploration.

## Keep Scope Small

Do not build all dream environments first.

Do not explain the full backstory yet.

Do not add final endings yet.

Focus on one complete loop that feels good, unsettling, and clear.
